# Best-Value Used SUVs in Canada Under $25,000 CAD (Model Years 2017–2022)
For Canadian shoppers, a used SUV in the $20,000–$25,000 CAD range from the 2017–2022 model years represents a sweet spot: most models have shed roughly one-third to half of their original sticker price, the worst depreciation has already happened, and many are still young enough to be reliable, safe, and reasonably efficient. Below are the ten SUVs that offer the strongest price-to-quality ratio for Canadian conditions. They are ranked broadly by overall value, but each excels in a different scenario.
## 1. Toyota RAV4 (2017–2019) — The Benchmark for Compact-SUV Value
The RAV4 is Canada's best-selling vehicle for good reason. The fourth-generation models (2017–2018) and the redesigned fifth-generation 2019 deliver legendary Toyota reliability, the strongest resale value in the segment, and an AWD system well-suited to Canadian winters. iSeeCars ranks the RAV4 first for 5-year resale value among small/compact SUVs (75% retained).
 * **Canadian price range:** $18,000–$25,000 for 2017–2019 AWD models. Note that in the current market, finding a clean, accident-free 2019 RAV4 AWD with under 80,000 km will push right against the $24,500–$25,000 hard limit.
 * **Reliability:** Above-average to excellent.
 * **Fuel economy (NRCan, AWD 2.5L):** 10.7/8.4 L/100 km city/highway for 2017–2018; the 2019+ redesign improves to roughly 9.2/7.0 L/100 km.
 * **Watch for:** The 8-speed automatic in the 2019 gas models generated complaints for low-speed hesitation and jerking; ensure the Transmission Control Module has received Toyota's software updates. If stretching the budget for a 2019 Hybrid, verify the status of the high-voltage cable due to "Cablegate" corrosion from road salt (Toyota has extended this warranty to 10 years). Confirm rust-proofing history on any Eastern-Canada example.
 * **Why it's #1 for value:** Excellent reliability, lowest depreciation in segment, parts availability everywhere, very strong cold-weather record, and the most plentiful supply in Canada.
## 2. Mazda CX-5 (2017–2020) — Best Quality Per Dollar
The CX-5 punches well above its price class for interior quality, handling, and reliability. RepairPal rates it 4.5/5 with an average annual repair cost well below the segment average.
 * **Canadian price range:** $17,000–$23,000 for 2017–2019 GS/GT AWD models; 2020 GS AWD with average mileage can sometimes be found just under $25,000.
 * **Reliability:** Excellent overall, but the 2018–2021 models carry a specific, severe risk regarding the engine casting.
 * **Fuel economy:** 10.2/8.2 L/100 km combined city/highway for 2.5L AWD.
 * **Watch for:** The 2.5L engine with cylinder deactivation (2018–2021) has a recognized casting defect that causes cylinder heads to crack, leading to oil leaks and a $4,000–$7,000 CAD repair. This is covered under Mazda TSB MC-10200939-0001. You must check the service history to see if the cylinder head has already been replaced with the updated 2022+ part. Also, watch for infotainment system glitches and rust on rear wheel arches in heavy salt regions.
 * **Why it's a great value:** Looks and drives like a near-luxury vehicle for mainstream prices; standard AWD optional on every trim.
## 3. Honda CR-V (2017–2020) — Practical, but Requires Careful Vetting
The CR-V is consistently a top-three compact SUV pick and retains 71% of its value over five years. However, Canadian buyers must navigate specific mechanical vulnerabilities.
 * **Canadian price range:** $17,500–$24,000 for 2017–2019 models with 90,000–150,000 km.
 * **Reliability:** Above average overall, but heavily dependent on how previous owners drove and maintained the 1.5L turbo.
 * **Fuel economy:** 1.5T AWD: 8.7/7.2 L/100 km; 2.4L LX AWD (2017 only): 9.4/7.6 L/100 km.
 * **Watch for:** Two major issues. First, "oil dilution" in cold-weather markets where fuel mixes with engine oil during short winter trips. Check the dipstick for a gasoline smell and confirm Honda's software TSB has been applied. Second, the 2017–2021 generation suffers from premature A/C compressor shaft seal leaks and extreme condenser vulnerability due to the wide-open lower grille. Test the A/C thoroughly and consider installing an aftermarket mesh guard to prevent rock punctures.
 * **Why it's still recommended:** Class-leading interior space, excellent visibility, IIHS Top Safety Pick.
## 4. Subaru Forester (2017–2020) — The Canadian-Winter Champion
Standard symmetrical AWD, generous ground clearance, and excellent visibility have made the Forester arguably the most winter-capable mainstream SUV available.
 * **Canadian price range:** $17,000–$24,000 for 2017–2019 models; the redesigned 2019 (fifth-gen) is mostly in the $21,000–$24,500 range.
 * **Reliability:** Mixed. Older head-gasket issues are gone, but the fifth-generation (2019+) introduced a severe new failure point.
 * **Fuel economy:** 9.7/7.5 L/100 km for the 2.5L non-turbo AWD.
 * **Watch for:** The 2019–2021 Forester (2.5L FB25DI engine) suffers from a notoriously high failure rate of the Thermal Control Valve (TCV). It leaks coolant internally, throwing the car into fail-safe mode and disabling the EyeSight system. Subaru issued a 15-year warranty extension (TSB 02-192-22R). Verify if the original TCV has been swapped for the redesigned part (#21319AA012). Also, watch for wheel bearings and occasional cracked windshields.
 * **Why it's a great value:** Unmatched all-weather confidence and excellent IIHS safety scores.
## 5. Hyundai Santa Fe / Santa Fe Sport (2017–2019) — Value with Caveats
The Santa Fe offers significant feature value, but buyers must be hyper-vigilant regarding widespread recalls and legal settlements.
 * **Canadian price range:** $14,000–$22,000 for 2017–2018 Santa Fe Sport AWD models, $20,000–$24,500 for the all-new 2019 Santa Fe.
 * **Reliability:** Average. While feature-rich, long-term powertrain and electrical reliability require strict vetting.
 * **Fuel economy:** Sport 2.4L AWD: about 11.5 L/100 km combined. The 2019 Santa Fe 2.4L AWD improves slightly to roughly 10.6 L/100 km combined.
 * **Watch for:** The ABS control module electrical short defect, which can cause engine compartment fires even when parked. Following a 2025 Canadian Supreme Court settlement, verifying the ABS module inspection and software update via the VIN is mandatory. Additionally, the 2.0T Theta II engine has a history of excessive oil consumption and rare bearing failures.
 * **Why it's good value:** Loaded standard features and the lowest typical price per feature in the segment.
## 6. Kia Sorento (2017–2020) — Best Cheap 3-Row Option
The Sorento is the most affordable way into a true (if tight) three-row SUV under $25,000. It is the mechanical twin of the Santa Fe XL.
 * **Canadian price range:** $14,000–$22,000 for 2017–2019 LX/EX V6 AWD models; some 2020s slip just under $25,000.
 * **Reliability:** Good, provided you opt for the correct powertrain.
 * **Fuel economy:** 3.3L V6 AWD: 12.5/9.4 L/100 km city/highway; 2.4L FWD: ~10.4 L/100 km combined.
 * **Watch for:** The exact same ABS module fire risk as the Hyundai models. Run the VIN to ensure the recall was completed. Avoid the 2.4L Theta II engine; the 3.3L V6 is significantly more durable and capable.
 * **Why it's good value:** Three-row capability, V6 power, and strong safety scores at an aggressive price point.
## 7. Toyota Highlander (2017–2018) — Premium 3-Row Reliability
Within the $25,000 ceiling, you can buy a 2017 LE V6 AWD or a 2018 LE AWD with 130,000–180,000 km. Because of its legendary durability, a Highlander can easily run past 300,000 km with routine maintenance.
 * **Canadian price range:** $20,000–$24,900 for 2017 LE V6 AWD with 130,000–180,000 km; 2018s typically stretch over the budget.
 * **Reliability:** Excellent — among the most reliable three-row SUVs available globally.
 * **Fuel economy:** 3.5L V6 AWD: 11.8/8.5 L/100 km.
 * **Watch for:** Higher mileage at this price point. Get a pre-purchase inspection covering brakes, suspension bushings, and the rear differential.
 * **Why it's good value:** Lowest projected long-term cost of ownership in the three-row segment.
## 8. Chevrolet Equinox / GMC Terrain (2018–2020) — Overlooked Bargains
These Canadian-built twins perform exceptionally well in long-term dependability studies but lack the premium pricing of Toyota or Honda, making them strong value plays.
 * **Canadian price range:** $14,000–$21,000 for 2018–2020 LT/SLE 1.5T AWD; 2019 Premier/SLT trims can be found in the low $20,000s.
 * **Reliability:** High marks for the 1.5T powertrain.
 * **Fuel economy:** 1.5T AWD: 9.7/7.7 L/100 km — among the best in the gas-only compact-SUV segment.
 * **Watch for:** Some 1.5T owners report timing-chain stretching and turbo cooling-line leaks past 150,000 km. Brake wear is faster than Japanese rivals. Confirm GM has performed any active recalls.
 * **Why it's good value:** You typically pay $2,000–$4,000 less than a comparable RAV4 or CR-V for similar reliability and better fuel economy.
## 9. Hyundai Tucson (2019–2021) — Loaded for the Money
Used Tucsons come heavily equipped for prices well below comparable Japanese models, provided buyers navigate the recall history.
 * **Canadian price range:** $15,000–$22,000 for 2019–2020 Preferred/Luxury AWD models; some 2021s appear just under $25,000.
 * **Reliability:** Good, assuming recalls are completed. The 2019+ redesign abandoned the problematic dual-clutch transmission for a reliable 6-speed automatic.
 * **Fuel economy:** 2.4L AWD: 10.7/8.5 L/100 km.
 * **Watch for:** The same ABS module electrical short (fire risk) that triggered the 2025 class-action settlement across Hyundai/Kia. Do not purchase without verifying this recall has been handled.
 * **Why it's good value:** Best feature-per-dollar in the segment.
## 10. Mazda CX-9 (2017–2019) — Best-Looking 3-Row Bargain
The CX-9 offers near-luxury interior quality and engaging driving dynamics for buyers who need a three-row SUV but dislike minivan-like handling.
 * **Canadian price range:** $20,000–$24,800 for 2017–2018 GS-L/GT AWD models with 90,000–150,000 km.
 * **Reliability:** Strong overall, with a durable 2.5T powertrain.
 * **Fuel economy:** 2.5T AWD: 11.6/9.1 L/100 km — strong for a 3-row.
 * **Watch for:** The third row is the tightest on this list. Check suspension bushings on rougher Canadian roads, and ensure the timing chain is healthy if mileage is high.
 * **Why it's good value:** High-output turbo runs on regular fuel; excellent interior material quality.

## Essential Canadian-Specific Buying Notes & Resources
**Mandatory Recall Checks:** Always run the vehicle's VIN through the **Transport Canada Motor Vehicle Safety Recalls Database**. Verifying the completion of the Subaru Forester TCV extension, Hyundai/Kia ABS module update, and Honda CR-V oil-dilution fixes is non-negotiable.
**Valuation and Pricing:** Finding pristine, low-mileage top-tier models (RAV4, CR-V) under $25,000 is increasingly difficult. Expect to compromise on trim level or accept mileage closer to 100,000 km. Always run the specific trim and mileage through the **Canadian Black Book** value calculator before negotiating.
**Winter Capability & Rust:** Subaru Forester leads for AWD capability, followed by the RAV4 and Highlander. Regardless of the brand, salt-belt buyers (Ontario, Quebec, Atlantic Canada) must inspect rear sub-frames, brake lines, and fuel-tank straps. The **Automobile Protection Association (APA)** provides excellent Canadian-specific data on rust resistance and hidden warranties.
**Fuel Economy Verification:** Do not rely on converted US MPG figures found on American forums. Use **Transport Canada's Fuel Consumption Ratings Search Tool** for the most accurate L/100 km ratings.
**Final Buying Tips:**
 1. Pay $130–$200 for an independent pre-purchase inspection.
 2. Pull a CARFAX Canada history to check for accident severity and salt-province exposure.
 3. Accept the 5–10% Canadian premium for Toyota and Honda models; their unmatched resale value recovers that cost upon eventual sale.
